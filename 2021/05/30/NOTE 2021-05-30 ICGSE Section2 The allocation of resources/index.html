Section2 The allocation of resources

Posted on 2021-05-30

Market

Demand

  • Definition: Demand is the willingness and ability to buy a product

  • Law of demand: Quantity demanded and price are inversely related. When the price falls, the demand rises.(Normal Goods)

  • Differentiate

    • Quantity demanded

      • Actual quantity at a certain price

    • Demand

      • Not a quantity but all the relation between price and quantity demanded

  • Determinations

    • Price

      • Change in quantity demanded

        • Extension in demand

          • Definition: Movement along the demand curve when quantity demanded increases due to a fall in the price of the product itself.

        • Contraction in demand

          • Definition: Movement along the demand curve when quantity demanded decreases due to an increase in the price of the product itself.

    • Non-price

      • Change in demand

        • Increase

          • Definition: a rise in quantity demanded at any given price, causing the demand curve to shift to the right

        • Decrease

          • Definition: a decrease in quantity demanded at any given price, causing the demand curve to shift to the left

      • Price of related goods

        • Substitutes

          • Definition: It is a product that can be used in place of another. They can satisfy the same want
          • price of substitute increase–>demand for substitute contract–>demand for the product increase–>price of the product increase

        • Complements

          • Definition: It is a product that is used together with another product. Complements are in joint
          • price of complement increase–>demand for complement contract–>demand for the product reduce–>price of the product decrease

      • Income

        • Normal goods

          • When income increases, the demand for it increases

        • Inferior goods

          • When income decrease, the demand for it increases

      • Successful advertising

        • More heavily, more demand

      • Interest rate

        • An increase in interest rate creates incentive for consumers to save more and purchase less, so the demand decreases

      • Population

        • An increase in population, an increase in demand

      • Fashion, taste, habit

      • Other factors

        • change in weather conditions
        • expectation
        • special events

Supply

  • Definition: It is the willingness and ability of firms to produce and sell a product.

  • Law of supply: Quantity supplied and price are directly related. When the price falls, the supply rises.

  • Differentiate

    • Quantity supply

      • Actual quantity at a certain price

    • Supply

      • Not a quantity but all the relation between price and quantity supplied

  • Determinations

    • Price

      • Change in quantity supplied

        • Extension in supply

          • Definition: Movement along the supply curve when quantity supplied increases due to a rise in the price of the product itself.

        • Contraction in supply

          • Definition: Movement along the supply curve when quantity supplied decreases due to a decrease in the price of the product itself.

    • Non-price

      • Change in supply

        • Increase

          • Definition: a rise in quantity supplied at any given price, causing the supply curve to shift to the right

        • Decrease

          • Definition: a decrease in supplied demanded at any given price, causing the supply curve to shift to the left

      • Costs of production

        • An increase in the costs of production results in an decrease in supply(want a higher price for it)

      • Improvement in technology

        • raise productivity, reduce costs of production, increase supply

      • Taxes

        • Only direct taxes on firms including corporation taxes, and indirect tax including VAT and excise duty
        • an increase in tax–>more expensive to supply a product and reduce supply

      • Subsidies

        • an increase in subsidy–>cheaper to supply, decrease in costs of production and increase supply

      • Price of other products

        • firms may choose to produce more other goods produced by the firms as an increase in other goods’ price and supply(opportunity cost)

      • Other factors

        • weather conditions and health of livestock and crops
        • disasters and wars
        • discoveries and depletions of commodities

Price determination

  • Definition: Equilibrium price is the price where quantity demanded and quantity supplied are equal. There is no pressure to change the price.

  • Disquilibrium

    • Definition: It is the situation where quantity demanded and quantity supplied are not equal

    • Surplus(Excess supply)

      • Definition: It is a situation where quantity demanded is greater than quantity supplied

    • Shortage(Excess demand)

      • Definition: It is a situation where quantity supplied is than quantity demanded

Price change

  • Equilibrium price increases

    • Demand increases

      • Quantity increases

    • Supply decreases

      • Quantity decreases

  • Equilibrium price decrease

    • Demand decreases

      • Quantity decreases

    • Supply increases

      • Quantity increases

Elasticity

PED

  • Definition: It measures the extent to which the quantity demanded changes when the price of the product changes

  • Formula: PED=percentage change in quantity demanded/percentage change in price

    • Sign

      • In the vast majority of cases, it is minus.

    • Size

      • This indicates the extent by which demand will extend or contract when price changes

  • Classification

    • Elastic demand

      • It occurs when a change in price results in a greater percentage change in quantity demanded
      • 1<PED<infinity
      • It is illustrated by a shallow curve
      • An increase in price results in a fall in revenue

    • Inelastic demand

      • It occurs when a change in price results in a smaller percentage change in quantity demanded
      • 0<PED<1
      • It is illustrated by a steep curve
      • An increase in price results in an increase in revenue

    • Perfectly elastic demand

      • It occurs when a change in price causes a complete change in the quantity demanded
      • PED=infinity
      • It is illustrated with a horizontal curve
      • An increase in price results in a fall in revenue

    • Perfectly inelastic demand

      • It occurs when a change in price causes on change in the quantity demanded
      • PED=0
      • It is illustrated with a vertical curve
      • An increase in price results in an increase in revenue

    • Unit elasticity of demand

      • It occurs when a change in price causes an equal change in the quantity demanded, leaving the total revenue unchanged
      • PED=1
      • An increase in price has no effect on revenue

  • Determination

    • Degree of necessity

      • Necessities tend to have a relative inelastic demand

    • Availability of close substitutes

      • Available substitutes result in an elastic demand

    • Proportion of income

      • A high proportion of income tends to result in elastic demand

    • Time period under consideration

      • In long run, consumers have more time to change habit, so it results in elastic demand

    • Addictive goods

      • Addictive goods tend to have an inelastic demand

  • Implications

    • Consumers

      • Benefit from lower prices and higher quality when demand is elastic

    • Producers

      • help them to decide on their pricing strategy
      • try to make the firm’s product more distinctive to make the demand for firm’s product more price inelastic and would give the producer more power to raise price

    • Governments

      • help them make a decision on its subsidy and taxation policies
      • discouraging consumption is more likely to be successful if demand is elastic

PES

  • Definition: It measures the extent to which the quantity supplied changes when the price of the product changes

  • Formula: PES=percentage change in quantity supplied/percentage change in price

    • Sign

      • It is a positive figure

    • Size

      • This indicates the degree of responsiveness of the quantity supplied to a change in price

  • Classification

    • Elastic supply

      • It occurs when the quantity supplied changes by a greater percentage than the change in price
      • 1<PES<infinity
      • It is illustrated by a shallow curve

    • Inelastic supply

      • It occurs when the quantity supplied changes by a smaller percentage than the change in price
      • 0<PES<1
      • It is illustrated by a steep curve

    • Perfectly elastic supply

      • It occurs when a change in price causes a complete change in quantity supplied
      • PES=infinity
      • It is illustrated by a horizontal curve

    • Perfectly inelastic supply

      • It occurs when a change in price has no effect on the quantity supplied
      • PES=0
      • It is illustrated by a vertical curve

    • Unit PES

      • It occurs when a change in price causes an equal percentage change in the quantity supplied

  • Determination

    • Time taken to produce it

      • If it takes a short time producing, the supply is more likely to be price elastic. It is more likely to be price elastic in long run.

    • Number of producers in the industry

      • More suppliers in the industry results in a more elastic supply

    • Feasibility of storing it

      • A higher level of stocks of finished goods results in the supply being more elastic

  • Implications

    • Consumers

      • benefit from supply being elastic

    • Producers

      • benefit from supply being elastic

    • Governments

      • benefit from supply being elastic

Economic system

Three fundamental questions

  • What goods and services should be produced? (Three sectors)
  • How should the goods and services be produced?
  • For whom should get the goods and services produced?

Sectors

  • Public sector

    • It is controlled by the government. It covers government run services and state-owned enterprises

  • Private sector

    • It covers business organisations which are owned by shareholders or individuals. These organisation respond to changes in market forces and are profit motivated

Planned Economic

  • Definition: It is an economic system where the government makes the crucial decisions, land and capital are state-owned and resources are allocated by government

Market Economic

  • Basic information

    • Definition: It is an economic system where consumers determine what is produced, resources are allocated by the price mechanism and land and capital are privately owned.

    • Intensive

      • Capital-intensive
      • Labour-intensive

    • Feature

      • Consumer sovereignty
      • Government intervention is minimal
      • Price mechanism

  • Advantages

    • Competitions(to consumers)

      • The nature of a market system determines the levels of competition

      • Advantages

        • Consumer sovereignty
        • Lower prices
        • High quality
        • Many choices

    • Incentives

      • To gain profits and avoid losses(firms)–>try to meet consumers’ needs and wants in order to obtain and maintain consumers
      • To receive high incomes(workers)–>develop skills, work hard, take more responsibility, be more mobile

    • Allocative efficiency（quantity)

      • Firm produce the products that consumers demand in the right quantities
      • Market forces, by changing prices, should eliminate shortages and surpluses and move market towards allocative efficiency.
      • (a competitive market)have both incentive in the form of profit and a threat of punishment in the form of a risk of going out business

    • Production efficiency(Average cost)

      • It produces at the lowest possible average cost
      • (a competitive market)have both incentive and a threat of punishment
      • Producing on PPC

    • Dynamic efficiency(Average costs continue to decrease)

      • The efficiency occurring over time as a result of investment and innovation
      • profit incentive and threat of going out of business

  • Disadvantages

    • Market failure

      • Underproduction(shortage)

      • Overproduction(surplus)

      • Definition: It occurs when market forces of demand and supply fail to produce the right products to the consumers in the right amount and at the lowest cost.(Seen as allocative inefficency)

      • Classification

        • Externality

          • It is the consumption and production of some products may affect third parties(social benefit>private benefit or social cost>private cost)
          • Definition: Social costs are the total costs to a society of an economic activity. Private costs are the costs borne by those directly consuming or producing a product. External costs are costs imposed on third parties
          • Definition: Social benefits are the total benefits to a society of an economic activity. Private benefits are the benefits received by those directly consuming or producing a product. External benefits are benefits enjoyed by the third parties
          • Formula: social=private+external

        • Information failure

          • Needed

            • Consumer

              • nature of the products and the benefits they can receive form them and their prices

            • Workers

              • the jobs, locations, qualifications required, remuneration and the nature of jobs for which their skills are best suited

            • Producers

              • consumer demand, raw material with good quality and lowest prices, most cost-efficient production methods

          • It occurs when there may be a lack of information or inaccurate information.(also called asymmetric information)

        • Merit/Demerit goods

          • Merit

            • Definition: It is the products which the government considers consumers do not fully appreciate how beneficial they are and so which will be under-consumed if left to market forces(positive externality)
            • Solution: Providing subsidy to produces; Government provision; Providing information; Financial incentive for consumers

          • Demerit

            • Definition: It is the products which the governments consider consumers do not fully appreciate how harmful they are and so which will be over-consumed if left to market forces(negative externality)
            • Solution: Increase taxes; Increase advertisement against consumption; Law to limit consumption; Limit import

        • Public goods

          • Features

            • Non-excludability
            • Non-rivalry

        • Abuse of monopoly power

          • Monopoly; Oligopoly; Monopolistic Competitive
          • No choice but to buy even at high prices
          • Solution: Remove entry barriers; Making uncompetitive practices illegal; Stop firms from merging; Give subsidies to other competition firms; Implement anti-monopoly law

        • Immobility of resouces

          • Occupational immobility
          • Geographical immobility
          • Solution: Improve education and provide training; Provide investment grants; Make it easier to but or rent housing in areas where has a high demand for labour

        • Short termism

          • Market forces may not allocate resources efficiently to enable future generations to be able to enjoy them.

    • Productive inefficiency

      • Producing inside PPC
      • losing market share and profits

    • Dynamic inefficiency

      • Do not do research and development
      • Being driven out of the market

Mixed Economic

  • Basic information

    • Definition: It is an economy in which both the private and public sectors play an important role
    • The degree of public and private sectors is determined by the government
    • Essential services are provided by public sector. Governments exist to redistribute income and wealth
    • In private sector, profit motives the firms to provide goods and services

  • Advantages

    • Eliminate externalities
    • Encourage consumption of merit goods
    • Discourage consumption of demerit goods
    • Provide public goods
    • Prevent high prices(ignore monopoly)
    • Make maximum use of resources
    • A more even distribution of income and wealth

  • Disadvantages(Government failure)

    • Overestimate the extent of private benefits; Difficult to calculate the most efficient quantity of public goods to supply
    • Take time to make decisions; Decisions influenced by political factors
    • Reduce economic efficiency by reducing incentives

  • Actions taken to solve market failure

    • Price ways

      • Maximun prices
      • Minimum prices

    • Non-price ways

      • Indirect taxation

        • Levy an indirect tax on demerit goods to decrease supply in order to reducing demand for these products

      • Subsidies

        • Provide a subsidy to increase supply to encourage the consumption of certain goods and services

      • Regulations

        • Definition: Regulations are rules which are designed to influence the behaviour of firms

      • Educations

      • Nationalisation

        • Definition: It is moving the ownership and control of an industry from the private sector to the government

        • Advantages

          • Decisions on the full costs and benefits involved
          • Be used to influence economic activity
          • Government monopoly will not abuse market power
          • Make planning and coordination easier
          • Ensure basic industries to survive, charge low prices and produce good quality

        • Disadvantages

          • Difficult to manage and control(DOS)
          • Inefficient, produce low quality products and charge relatively high prices, due to a lack of competition and the knowledge that they cannot go bankrupt
          • Need to be subsidised if making loss(OC of government revenue)

      • Privatisation

        • Definition: It is moving the ownership and control of an industry from the government to the private sector

        • Advantages

          • The ability to earn one-off privatisation proceeds from the sale of state-owned assets.
          • Reduce government debts(No longer has to maintain the operation; earns revenue from selling the business; earns taxes)
          • Reduce costs to taxpayers
          • Incentive to improve efficiency, risking taking and innovation

        • Disadvantages

          • Consumers may face higher prices
          • To protect the public interest, privatisation may still require government regulation and intervention.
          • OC（State-owned enterprises provide social benefits)

      • Direct provision

        • Definition: Governments provide certain goods and services free of charge to their citizens

        • Advantages

          • The goods and services are accessible to all people in society
          • private benefits for the individual and external benefits for third parties in society

        • Disadvantages

          • OC
          • Free goods and services may be over-consumed
          • In the case of a shortage of supply caused by excess demand, it can be difficult to decide who should be able to take advantage of the free government service.
          • Free riders take advantage of free goods and services without contributing to government revenue by paying taxes.

Economy

Economy is that people and firms produce, exchange and consume goods and services

Microeconomics is the study of the behaviour and decisions of households and firms and the performance of individual markets.

Macroeconomics is the study of the whole economy.

Decision makers

  • Also called economic agents

  • Household

    • including consumers, savers and workers
    • Consumers aim at low price and good quality products
    • Workers aim at good working conditions and high pay
    • Savers aim at their money to be safe and to give a good return

  • Firm

    • They are the business concerns that produce goods and services and employ workers and other FOPs
    • Firms(in private sector)aim at making as much profit as possible

  • Government

    • It is the system which rules a country or region. It produces and provides some products, financial benefits, and levies taxes on and regulates the private sector
    • Government aims at a strong economy and improve the performance of individual markets
