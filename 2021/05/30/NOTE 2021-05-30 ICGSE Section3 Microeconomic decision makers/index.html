Results of growth

  • Economies of scale(Advantages)

    • Definition: It is the cost-saving benefits of large-scale of operations which reduce average costs of production

    • Internal economies of scale

      • Average costs fall along the LRAC curve, and reach an optimum point.

      • Types

        • Marketing economies

          • Buying economies

            • Receive a discount when buying raw materials in bulk and placing large orders for capitals, even in terms of better quality and the speed of delivery. Pay less for each item purchased

          • Selling economies

            • Total costs of processing orders and some other processes do not rise in line with the number of orders.
            • Reduce advertising costs. The total costs can be spread over more units and discounts may be secured.

        • Managerial economies

          • Large firms can afford to employ specialist staff as they can spread the pay over a high number of units. This can increase efficiency, reduce costs of production and raise demand and revenue

        • Technical economies

          • Larger the output, more viable to use large technologically advanced machinery to increase efficiency and decrease AVC

        • Financial economies

          • Large firms will find it easier and cheaper to raise finance as banks are more willing to lend to them and charge lower interests

        • Research and development economies

          • Large firms can have a R&D department to reduce costs by developing more efficient production methods and new products

        • Risk bearing economies

          • Large firms produce a range of products to spread risks of trading(shift resources to other products when the profitability of one product falls)

        • Labour economies

          • Large firms can engage in division of labour among their own staff

    • External economies of scale

      • A firmâ€™s average costs will be reduced not by changes in the firmâ€™s output, but by changes in its industryâ€™s output.(A downward shift of a firmâ€™s LRAC curve)

      • Types

        • A skilled labour force

          • A firm can recruit workers who have been trained by other firms in the industry

        • A good reputation

          • An area may gain a reputation for high quality production

        • Specialist suppliers of raw materials and capital goods

          • When an industry becomes large enough, ancillary industry may be set up to provide for the needs of the industry

        • Specialist markets

          • Universities may run courses for workers in large industries. Bank and transport industry may provide services specially designed to meet the particular needs of firms

        • Specialist markets

          • Some large industries have specialist selling places and arrangements

        • Improved infrastructures

          • The growth of an industry may encourage a government and private sector firs to provide better road links and electricity supplies, build new airports and develop dock facilities

    • Definition: It occurs when the average costs of production start to increase as the size of a firm increase

      • Average costs increase along the LRAC curve starting from the optimum point

            • Total

              • Definition: Variable costs are payment of production that change when the level of output changes
              • As output increases, total variable cost rises. It usually tends to rise slowly at first and then rise more rapidly because of economies of scale and diseconomies of scale
              • A line with positive gradient but not straight

            • Average

              • Definition: Average variable costs are total variable costs divided by output
              • A U-shape curve because of economies and diseconomies of scale

    • Revenue

      • Total

        • Definition: It is the total amount of money received by firms receive through sale of their products and services. Formula: TR=Q*P

      • Average

        • Definition: It is the typical price received from the sale of a good or service. It is same as the price

Objectives

  • Survival

    • At the beginning of a business, their initial objective may be survival
    • During difficult times when demand is falling, large firms may have survival as objectives

  • Growth

    • An increase in size may bring advantages like economies of scale
    • Those who run firms may want the growth as their pay and status may be closely linked to the size of the firm and increase gob security
    • If growth is achieved by merging, competition will be reduced and the company may gain a larger market share

  • Social welfare

    • Public sector companies may aim at improving social welfare through charging relative low prices to ensure they are affordable. Also, they may get to understand the externalities
    • Some private sector firms also show concern about environmental and social effects of their actions.

  • Profit satisficing

    • Make enough dividends to keep shareholders happy while pursuing other objectives

  • Profit maximisation

    • Other objectives may conflict with this, but pursuit of them may actually increase profits in the long run.

    • Formula: profit=revenue-cost; It is maximised when the gap between total revenue and total costs is the highest

    • Effect of changes in profit

      • Provide an incentive with entrepreneurs to undertake production
      • Provide firms with more finance to update their capital equipment and expand their business
      • Make it easier to obtain external finance
      • Easier to recruit top managers and directors, attracted by their success
      • A fall in profit may have little impact on the behaviour of firms in the short run. But in the long run, firms may cut back on production and cease production

    • Ways of increasing profit

      • Reduce costs of production

        • Reduce any wastages and inefficiency
        • Increase the productivity of factors of production
        • Increase the size of the firm to enjoy economies of scale or stronger market power to make demand be inelastic

      • Raise revenue

        • Improve the quality of their products, diversify and be more responsive to changes in consumer demand
        • Increase output
        • Advertising

Trade unions

Definition: Trade unions are organisations that promote and protect the interests of their members(employees) to improve their wages and working conditions

Classification

  • Craft unions

    • These represents workers with particular skills. These workers may be employed in a number of industries

  • General unions

    • These unions include workers with a range of skills and from a range of industries.(usually have a large membership of unskilled workers)

  • Industrial unions

    • These seeks to represent all the workers in a particular industry

  • White-collar unions

    • These unions represent particular professions

Functions

  • Bargaining with employers for pay rises

    • Basis of wage claims

      • Work harder and increase productivity
      • An industry with higher profits can afford to pay higher wages.(Workers are likely to have contributed to the higher profits)
      • Comparability argument. Workers in comparable occupations who have received a wage increase
      • A rise in the cost of living due to inflation which reduces the real wages. A wage rise is needed to meet the increased of cost of living

  • Negotiate on behalf of their members on job security, working hours and working conditions.

  • Protect or improve workersâ€™ rights

  • Provide information on a range of issues for their members

  • Help with education and training schemes

  • Participate in measures designed to increase demand for the product produced

  • Ensure members are given legal advice when necessary

  • Give support to members when they are made redundant

  • Provide financial and legal support to workers who may have been unfairly dismissed or disciplined

  • Pressuring governments to adopt legislation in favour of workers

Factors affecting the strength of a trade union

  • A high number of members

    • The more the members a union has, the more funds it has to finance its activities.
    • It is difficult to replace union labour by non-union labour.

  • A high level of skills

    • Unions with skilled workers are in a relative strong position as it is difficult to replace skilled workers with other skilled workers and expensive to train unskilled workers

  • A consistent demand for product produced by the workers

    • Unions that represent workers making essential goods and services are in strong position to bargain

  • Favourable government legislation

    • If the governments allow industrial action, the trade union will be in a stronger position

  • A high level of economic activity

    • If output and income in a country increase, more industries may perform well, so wages and non-wage factors may improve. Also, when output reaches high levels and most people are employed, firms may compete for workers. So bargaining power increases

Ways

  • Collective bargaining

    • Definition: The trade union acts as a means of communication and negotiation between employers and employees through a process called collective bargaining

  • Industrial actions

    • Overtime ban

      • Workers refuse to work more than their normal hours

    • Work to rule

      • Workers deliberately slow down production by complying rigidly with every rule and regulation

    • Go-slow

      • Workers carry out tasks deliberately slowly to reduce production

    • Strike

      • Workers refuse to work and may also protest or picket outside their workplace to stop deliveries and prevent non-unionised workers from entering

Consequences

  • Advantages

    • Provide a channel of communication between employers and employees. This makes firms less time consuming, less stressful and cheaper to negotiate with workers as a group
    • They encourage workers to engage in education and training to raise productivity
    • Provide workers with an outlet to vent discontent and channel their grievances to the employers to reduce conflict
    • Benefit non-unionised workers with higher wages and better working conditions

  • Disadvantages

    • Taking industrial action may result in lost productivity
    • Increase the costs of production and reduce profits. This will cause the government revenue decreases as well
