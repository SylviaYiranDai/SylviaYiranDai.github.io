ISSUE 1 Should the compensation of executives be subject to government regulations?

Introduction

It is common that a worker’s compensation may reflect that worker’s marginal revenue product which is defined as the additional revenues or reduced costs generated by the employment of an additional worker. The higher the productivity, the greater should be his or her earnings.

However, there are some other determinants of earning, known as abstract notions of worth and value.

I still remember that last semester when we did a business project which aimed at analysing a company, we were asked to analyse their unethical behaviours. Starbucks which is my choice does have many unethical behaviours, including low wages in plantation and tax avoidance. Also, it has the problem of excessive directors’ pay. Why the CAIE Business regards excessive pay as an unethical behaviour? This is mainly because the directors may not produce as much value as their pay to the whole company. If they do not, they are exactly taking other workers’ pay maybe the employers in office even the workers at the production line. Meanwhile, they may do tax avoidance in order to keep higher percentage of their earnings, this is not only unethical but also illegal.

As a result, the key problem may be whether the directors are exactly making enough benefits to the company which is worth rewarding as much high earnings as they receive or just receive high wages without achieving that.

When looking at the government, there is no doubt that free labour market without regulations from the government is a good condition. However, market failure may appear. On the other hand, if government sets a wage ceiling for directors, this may create disincentive for them to increase their productivity.

To sum up, there are probably two conflicts in this issue, including the discussion about whether it is proper for them to receive high wages and the discussion about whether the advantages of government regulation overweigh disadvantages.

YES

Our financial system is making fail as it created risks, does not create financial products which would help ordinary Americans face the important risks that you confronted, and mismanaging capital using high transaction costs. Also, These problems lead the economy to brink of ruin.

However, it is very sad that the pay was not connected with social returns or even long-run profitability of the sector. If they do connect, it is appropriate. Also, in financial system, both individual and institutional incentives were misaligned compared with other market economies.

Incentives which play important role in the market economy is discussed. They took the easier route which is increasing risks compared with outperforming the market in order to receive higher returns. It was this excessive risk taking that help helped bring capitalism to the brink. Meanwhile, there is a problem caused by incentive which is people pay more attention to the quantity instead of quality due to quantity-based payment.

Flawed incentives affect incentives to innovate. A better alignment of private rewards and social returns and better regulations including regulations that affect incentive structures holds out the prospect of better innovations.

NO

Compensation structures have been responsible for excessive risk taking in the financial services industry. It showed that many companies paid high bonus to their employers after receiving Federal bailout funds. The compensation constraints currently on TARP recipients will likely destroy these organisations unless they can quickly repay the government and avoid the constraints. Regulating compensation in financial services will cripple one of USA most important and historically most productive industries.

Given the penalties for poor performance inherent in both cash and equity incentive plans, there is nothing inherent in the current structure of compensation in financial service firms that lead to obvious incentives to take excessive risks. If the government is very clear that there is no “Too Big to Fail” guarantee, there is no need for government oversight.

Except for the Bonus culture, inappropriate performance measurement can lead to risk taking. In order to solve this problem, companies may pay people to write “good loans” and penalise them for writing “bad loans”. However, the challenge is identifying a good loan without waiting up to 30 years.

Problem of compensation in financial services can be improved. Introducing and enforcing bonus bans or “clawback” provisions for recovery of rewards if and when there is future revision of critical indicators on which the rewards were based or received is one of the solutions. Also ensuring that bonuses are based on value creation is another one. However, government rules and regulations may not make a contribution to it. For example, Golden Parachute and Section 280(G) had opposite influence on the financial services compared with its original designed plan.

Conclusion

Joseph believed that financial crisis represented the failure of the financial system to perform two of its key roles, including managing risk and allocating capital. Also, he thought that it was hard to identify significant social benefits and easy to identify large social costs associated with some of the recent financial innovations. As mentioned above, the incentive structures misallocated the capital including the human capital. For example, some of American’s most talented young succumbed to the lure of easy money.

Kevin believes that there is little empirical evidence to support the charge that compensation systems led to excessive risk taking in the financial services industry. Also, it is easy to say that loan officers should be paid for making good loans and penalised for making bad loans, but proposed regulations go in the wrong direction by acquiring pay “ be based solely on objective measures of performance”. Meanwhile, it is highly unlikely that compensation practices can be improved through increased government rules and regulations.
