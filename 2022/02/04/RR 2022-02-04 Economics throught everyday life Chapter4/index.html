PartA Competitive versus non competitive markets

  • A market or industry characterised by perfect competition has so many sellers of a good or s
  • The products in a perfectly competition environment market, and even ini a market that is highly if not perfectly competitive, tend to be standardised.
  • If a firm is the only seller of a particular good or service, the firm is said to have a pure monopoly on that good or service.
  • A market can also be an oligopoly, one that consists of a few large firms or is dominated by a few large firms.

PartB Monopolies

  • One barrier to entry is when it’s most efficient for a single firm to supply an entire market. In this situation, the firm is said to have a natural monopoly.
  • Another type of barrier is a resource monopoly, with a single firm controlling all access to a necessary input.
  • Common misconceptions about monopolies
    • One misconception is that monopoly firms are guaranteed high profits.
      • It’s true, of course, that if a company has a market monopolised, the company stands to earn a much higher profit than it would if the market were competitive.
      • It’s also possible for a company to have a monopoly on a product or service for which there isn’t enough demand to generate a profit.
    • Another misconception is that a monopolist necessarily charges the very highest possible price for its product or service.
      • Because this monopoly firm wants to maximise its profit, it can’t charge the highest price that a tiny minority of its customers would be willing to pay. If it did, it would lose the vast majority of the market. The firm’s customers would simply find other alternatives.
    • One of the biggest misconceptions about monopolies is that they’re always bad.
      • But the fact is that many monopolies create extraordinary value, not just for stockholders, managers, and employees, but for society as a whole.
      • Besides, some products might never have become available at all if their inventors and developers hadn’t been granted patents and monopoly power for long enough to recoup the costs of research and development.
  • Identifying monopolies
    • There are measures that economists can use to determine how competitive an industry is. The two principle measures are concentration ratios and the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index(HHI).
    • “Four-firm concentration ratio”
      • When the ‘four-firm concentration ratio’ of an industry is calculated, the result shows the market share of the four largest firms in an industry as a proportion of the industry’s total sales.
    • HHI
      • The HHI’s potential values may be as low as nearly zero (for a very highly competitive industry) and as high as 10000 (for an industry whose market is limited to one seller, a purely monopolistic scenario).
      • If a proposed merger is a highly concentrated market seemed likely to increase the HHI by more than 200 points, then the merger might face government opposition on the grounds that it would unduly decrease competition if allowed to proceed.
    • We’ve seen that every firm in an oligopolistic market is extremely sensitive to the effects of the actions taken by the other firms in that market.
    • A group of businesses engaged in collusion is commonly referred to as a cartel.
  • Monopolistic competitions
    • The monopolistic competition does enjoy a monopoly, but only when it comes to its own brand; in contrast to the pure monopolist, and there are close substitutes for the monopolistic competitor’s product.
    • A monopolistic competitor’s goal is to convince consumers that there really are no close substitutes for its product, even though that’s actually not the case.

PartC Antitrust regulation and enforcement

  • The government has long sought both to regulate firms that have monopoly power and, at times, to restrict the formation of monopolies in the first place.
  • The economic justification for regulating a monopoly is that monopolistic practices tend to create higher prices and lower levels of output than would exist in a competitive market, and such outcomes aren’t socially optimal.
  • Review of mergers and acquisitions
    • One of the primary ways for the government to limit the formation of monopolies is to review proposed mergers and not allow some of them to go through.
    • Merger cases can become very political.
    • The fact that some administrations have allowed mergers that probably would have failed under other administrations implies that a certain amount of discretion exists in the enforcement of antitrust laws.
  • Price-based antitrust regulation
    • The government may choose to regulate a monopoly firm by imposing a price ceiling with the aim of achieving a market price close or equal to the price that would prevail in a competitive market.
    • In a highly concentrated market, however, it’s hard to get a price ceiling right because they must gauge what the competitive price would be in a market that has never been truly competitive.
    • Government regulators may also prevent a dominant firm from engaging in certain kinds of anticompetitive behaviour such as predatory pricing, or the practice of setting a very low price, often below the cost of producing a good, so as to har a competitor.

Personal thinking

At the beginning of this chapter, the author discussed several forms of market structures and then mentioned the misconception of monopoly, followed by a variety of forms of antitrust actions. Before reading this, I have never learn the antitrust acts so detailedly and structurally.

Some of the people in our world believe that the monopoly is bad or even evil. However, in fact, these monopolies do play important roles in the market and generate benefits to the whole society. We never deny that monopoly power brings problems such as a low degree of competition, a relatively high price, fewer choices, and the over reliance on certain companies. It is kind of critical thinking that it both has positive impacts and negative outcomes.

However, although there are great positive impacts creating by the monopolies, the governments all around the world are still trying to imposing regulations and laws to prevent some actions and even reduce the existence of monopoly. This is because the economists, the governments, the monopolistic companies, the customers and other stakeholders holds different views, all of who argue for their benefits or the benefits of the most people. This is acceptable after all.
