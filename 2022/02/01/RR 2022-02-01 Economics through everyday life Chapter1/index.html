Thinking on Economics Through Everyday Life Chapter1 What is economics & Why does it matter?

Posted on 2022-02-01

Extract

PartA Economics Defined

  • What do economists do? A simple answer is that economists practice, teach, or work to advance the field of economics.
  • Every person who chooses finance or accounting as a college major is also required to duty economics, but economics isn’t the same as finance or accounting. Economics is the framework that underlies all the business disciplines, just as physics is the framework that underlies all the engineering disciplines.
  • What interests an economist is how human beings behave when they get their hands on a pile of money. In other words, economics is about human behaviour.
  • We might say that economics is the social science concerned with how people (or other decision-making units such as business firms or government agencies) can best allocate their limited resources to achieve optimal or maximum satisfaction. Or, we could say that economics deals with how people make choices in conditions of scarcity.

PartB Economic Resources

  • Most modern economists start from the assumption that having more material goods is better than having fewer material goods. Thus the more-is-better paradigm is fundamental to economics, although it is sometimes questioned and even criticised.
  • Economic resources are generally divided into four categories: land, labour, capital, and entrepreneurship.
    • Land includes any natural asset that may be used in producing goods and services.
    • Labour is exactly workers along with their skills and abilities. The resource category of labour also has a connection with the term human capital, which refers to workers’ skills, experience, and education.
    • Capital includes all goods produced in support of the production of other goods.
    • Entrepreneurs assemble material resources and determine reduction and distribution. They are innovators who dream up new products, create new twists to old products, and open new markets for established products. They also assume the risk of failure inherent in any business endeavour.
  • When the stock of resources increases, or when the quality of resources improves, the result is economics growth.

PartC Schools and Subfields of Economics

  • No one is coordinating all this activity. It is all occurring as if it were being guided by an unseen power. This power is what Adam Smith called the market’s “invisible hand,” and what modern economists recognise as market forces.
  • Major schools of economics thought
    • Classical school
      • The economy operates best when markets are mostly left to their own devices — when the government employs a liaises-faire, or hands-off, approach.
      • Prices (also known as the price mechanism) allocate resource efficiently, as if all economic activity were being guided by an invisible hand.
      • Advocate free trade, specialisation, and the division of labour.
      • There was little or no role for the government in managing the economy.
    • Marxist school
      • Capital is will one day be replaced by socialism and then, later on, by communism.
      • Labour theory of value — It states that the value of a good derives from the labour required to produce it.
      • Marxists favour the notion of tightly controlled markets or no markets at all.
    • Neoclassical school
      • Built on the proposition of Classical economics and began to focus on some of the finer details of interactions in markets.
      • The neoclassical view begins with the assumption that people are basically rational when they make decisions.
      • Neoclassicals believe that consumers behave rationally and attempt to maximise their utility, or satisfaction, within the limits of their available resources. Sellers, also assumed to be acting rationally, are seen as attempting to maximise their profits.
      • The focus on comparing additional costs and additional benefits, or marginal analysis are emerged from the Neoclassical school.
    • Keynesian school
      • He agreed with a number of basic tenets of Classical economics. But he also disagreed with certain Classical ideas such as the notion that unemployment is self-correcting, or the belief that government has no significant role to play in correcting the economic problems of a nation, state, or region.
      • Keynes and his disciples believed that government has the means, as well as the moral obligation to counteract problems such as unemployment and inflation through its taxing and spending policies.
    • Austrian school
      • The Austrian school of economic thought is related to the Neoclassical school and sometimes even seen as a subcomponent of that school.
      • As believers in and defenders of free markets, the Austrians are vehemently opposed to government involvement in the economy.
    • New institutional school
      • The New Institutional school of economic thought is another extension of Neoclassical economics.
      • One important idea that emerged from New Institutional economics in the concept of transaction costs, or the extra costs involved in the completion of a transaction. These include the costs associated with searching for products, negotiating terms, and enforcing agreements.
    • Monetarist school
      • Monetarism is another school of economic thought that argues for free markets.
      • It maintains that, in the short term, the economy’s output, or gross domestic product (GDP) is primarily determined by the money supply, or the total amount of money in circulation. Over the longer periods (or what economist typically refer to as “the long run”) the key determinant of the current-dollar GDP is the overall prices in the economy.
    • Behaviouralist school
      • Behavioural economics, broadly speaking, borrows certain concepts and ideas from psychology and overlaps to some degree with that field.
      • Through carefully designed laboratory experiments and other forms of research, behavioural economists have identified situations in which people are systematically irrational.
      • Behavioural economics includes several different strands such as psychological economics, experimental economics, and behavioural finance.

Personal thinkings

This chapter mainly focuses on the most basic concepts in Economics, such as the factors of production and scarcity. Additionally, it introduces some major schools of economics thought including behaviouralist school, monetarist school, new institutional school, Austrian school, Keynesian school, Neoclassical school, Marxist school, and Classical school.

When I was reading the part for classification of schools, it was surprising that many of them occur in both IGCSE and A-Level textbooks without being mentioned the type of school. For example, I have never learnt it before that it was Neoclassical economists who argued that customers behaved rationally during purchasing. During the process of introducing all these major school, I started to understand that Economics is never only being black or white which means if we can explain it with our own words or with the support of data, we can say that our conclusion makes sense.

However, generating this understanding is much more difficult than just knowing it from the aspect of ration. When reading the Marxist school, I found it hard for me to criticise its sayings.

There is one sentence in the chapter which I really appreciate and love, saying, “Even Nobel laureates in economics sharply disagree with one another. That’s one reason why you, as an informed citizen, should have a basic understanding of what’s at stake in those disagreements, so that you can listen to both sides and arrive at your own conclusions.” The values of UWC (United World College) include “celebration of different”. From my point of view, this can be applied in the study and research of Economics as well.
